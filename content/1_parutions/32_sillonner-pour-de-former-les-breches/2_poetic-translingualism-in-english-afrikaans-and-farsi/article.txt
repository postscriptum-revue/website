text: <strong>Introduction</strong>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">In the spring of 2020, Khashayar Mohammadi invited Klara du Plessis to start a collaborative writing project. Knowing du Plessis’ translingual </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Ekke</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">, a poetry collection in Afrikaans and English, he had been researching possible similarities between Afrikaans and Farsi—their respective mother tongues—discovering that they indeed share a very prevalent G or χ sound. The two poets started playing in a shared Google doc, thinking that they would compose a handful of poems. The creative impetus of the collaboration was so strong, however, that they wrote the first draft of an entire trilingual poetry collection in English, Afrikaans, and Farsi in a matter of weeks. This manuscript is provisionally called </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Fricatives</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> and is scheduled for publication through Palimpsest Press in 2023.</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">Both Mohammadi and du Plessis</span> <span style="font-weight: 400;">are in the unique position of having more than one language in which they think and create. For him, it is English and Farsi, and for her it is English and Afrikaans. They live in and form part of a country (Canada) and literary community, however, that obviously has an extremely rich linguistic diversity, but only officially acknowledge English and French—in terms of publishing, in particular. Mirene Arsanios diagnoses this linguistic repression as a symptom of settler colonialism in her essay “Notes on Mother Tongues”: “The ‘nation’ of the modern, settler-colonial nation-state is premised on the eradication of [...] languages [...] it turns lands into territories that stand for a nation’s monolithic identity (nationalism) (monolingualism)” (2020, 2). In other words, Literature is beholden to nationhood in a way that can dictate the kinds of literatures that are allowed to exist, to be published, and to be read.</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">One form of resistance to this condition is translingualism—writing in and across languages, and deliberately including languages that the majority of a Canadian readership would not necessarily understand. Sarah Dowling writes in </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Translingual Poetics:</span></i>
<blockquote><span style="font-weight: 400;">I use the term </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">trans</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">lingual [...] because it describes the capacity of languages to interact, influence, and transform one another [...] Translingualism is generally understood as a set of strategies by which writers engage with diverse linguistic codes in ways that are context-dependent. Translingual practice does not simply flout established norms and dominant institutions, but negotiates these social constraints in relation to writers’ competencies and repertoires [...] the term </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">translingual </span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">typically describes critical, oppositional, and survival practices (2018, 4-5).</span></blockquote>
<span style="font-weight: 400;">For both poets, coming to terms with the strength inherent to translingual poetic practice is a major part of their development as writers. They can write democratically across languages, allowing words to enter poems as they are inspired to do, without worrying whether a monolingual audience would </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">understand </span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">the work or not. Even amongst themselves, they also invite each other into their respective languages, creating an exophonic dialogue between languages; this act of hospitality into Farsi and Afrikaans, framed by a shared proficiency in English, allow the poets access into strange linguistic terrain that they themselves do not understand. Semantic comprehension is unimportant and what does gain currency is the productive disruption caused by bringing so-called minority languages into the hegemonic reading space of an anglophone audience. Each time they use an Afrikaans or Farsi word, they perforate the illusion of a stable and universal English.</span>
<p style="text-align: center;"><b>Lungscapes</b></p>
<span style="font-weight: 400;">The fricative reclines in its resonance</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">across languages and landscapes /</span>

<i><span style="font-weight: 400;">landskappe en tongskappe / </span></i>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">vibrating at the frequency of speech</span>

&nbsp;

<span style="font-weight: 400;">The thing about expansiveness</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">is that it never comes to an end / </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">einder </span></i>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">being a placeholder for the line of sight</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">circling around continents / consonants</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">thrill-seeking and trilling</span>

&nbsp;

<span style="font-weight: 400;">Each word sinks beneath the horizon</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">of its throat. Oral moon leaves one last ovum</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">glimmering in the nighttime stratum</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">of the sentence. Straddling grammar,</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">the egg / </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">eier</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> / </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">eie </span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">/ </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">ei </span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">/ staggers inside its own</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">weight, the shell needling its decline</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">with the technology of a crack</span>

&nbsp;

<span style="font-weight: 400;">Crack the whip of vocab. </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">Eina, </span></i>

<i><span style="font-weight: 400;">roep die sin </span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">/ sinful / </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">sonde </span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">/ </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">sondage. </span></i>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">See, a survey of sounds</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">shows a sensational reliance on similarity,</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">on familiarity, intimacy, coitus interruptus,</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">on a firm verbal handshake shivering</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">across the surface of poems, loanwords,</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">and dictionaries in all languages</span>

&nbsp;

<span style="font-weight: 400;">Dipping in and out of relevance /</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">reverb fricative / returns with daybreak / </span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">line break / crisps diction with gruff </span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">crackling of spikes, spittle, and frills</span>

&nbsp;

<span style="font-weight: 400;">To separate a sound into functionality,</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">then to reunite it with the gentle endlessness /</span>

<i><span style="font-weight: 400;">langskap einders </span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">/ of  lungscapes</span>

<p style="text-align: center;"><b>Aarie</b></p>
<i><span style="font-weight: 400;">vawhed</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> now </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">vawheh</span></i>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">from </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">toheed </span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">to </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">tohee</span></i>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">the tongue is prosthetic unity</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">pros- : </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">in addition to</span></i>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">+τίθημι : to put (literal act of laying down)</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">like </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> </span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">τῐ́θημῐ</span> <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%80%CE%BF%CF%8D%CF%82#Ancient_Greek"><span style="font-weight: 400;">πόδα</span></a> <span style="font-weight: 400;">: I plant the foot</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">τῐ́θημῐ </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> </span></i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BD%85%CF%80%CE%BB%CE%BF%CE%BD#Ancient_Greek"><span style="font-weight: 400;">τὰ ὅπλα</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">: </span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">I rest arms</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">I bear arms</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">I lay down arms and surrender</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">language is pros-θέσις</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">an addition seeking to fulfil</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">an absent limb</span> <span style="font-weight: 400;">itching</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">a primal map/ </span> <span style="font-weight: 400;">ejaculate from</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">a time-bruise on the throat</span>

<i><span style="font-weight: 400;">the last song beyond mankind</span></i>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">words as habitat for a thought system</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">systemic erasure of sounds echo in history</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">the k now silent in knife</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">the performative exploitation</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">of sounds lost to history</span>
